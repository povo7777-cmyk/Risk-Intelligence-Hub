Executive recommendation — business continuity

The BIA confirms that two critical scenarios carry no tested recovery plan: OT/SCADA compromise (MTPD 2 hours, no BCP documented) and trading platform total outage (MTPD 4 hours, last DR test over 18 months ago). Both link directly to the two highest-residual risks in the register. A regulatory inspection under DORA or NIS2 would flag these gaps immediately, representing a licence-to-operate risk in addition to operational and financial exposure.

The ISO 22301 maturity assessment shows the organisation is strongest on risk assessment and weakest on exercising and continual improvement — precisely the two pillars regulators examine most closely. The September 2026 tabletop exercise is necessary but insufficient — the OT/SCADA BCP must be documented before that exercise can be run meaningfully.

01
Document OT/SCADA BCP immediately
CISO to produce a documented BCP for OT/SCADA disruption by end Q3 2026. This is a DORA Article 11 obligation and a prerequisite for the September tabletop exercise.
02
Run trading failover DR test in Q3 2026
CTO to execute a full DR test of trading platform failover to secondary datacenter. Last test was over 18 months ago. The 4-hour RTO target must be validated, not assumed.
03
Embed BCM in supplier contracts
Procurement to add BCM and DR requirements to all Tier-1 supplier contracts at next renewal. ISO 22301 clause 8.4.3 compliance is required and currently absent from all vendor MSAs.
04
Elevate BCM to Board agenda Q3 2026
Head of ERM to present the BCM maturity assessment and gap closure plan to the Board Risk Committee in Q3 2026. NIS2 and DORA create personal liability for directors where BCM obligations are not met.

Executive recommendation — R-001 market & liquidity risk

The Monte Carlo analysis confirms that at the current 18-month forward curve of CHF 38/MWh the portfolio operates below its CHF 42/MWh breakeven. Under base assumptions the probability of a loss year exceeds 20% and the CVaR tail reaches deeply negative EBITDA territory. Price volatility of 35% p.a. is sufficient to generate outlier loss years even above breakeven, making mean-based budgeting unreliable. Management should anchor capital allocation and dividend planning to the VaR 95% outcome, not the expected value.

01
Execute forward hedging immediately
Lock 60% of generation 12 months forward. Reduces VaR by 30–50% — see Hedge Analyser for quantification.
02
Re-anchor budget to VaR 95%
Board and ExCo planning floors should use VaR 95% not expected EBITDA. Include 3-sigma price shock in stress scenario set.
03
Trigger impairment review at 60 days below breakeven
Activate asset impairment protocol when revenue coverage of fixed O&M falls below 1.1× for two consecutive quarterly reviews.

Executive recommendation — forward hedging strategy

A 60% forward hedge at a price above spot (default: CHF 42/MWh forward vs CHF 38/MWh spot) simultaneously reduces downside tail risk and adds expected value — the forward is locked above current market, making this a value-accretive risk transfer, not merely a cost. CVaR improvement is proportionally larger than VaR improvement — the hedge compresses severity of tail losses most dramatically in the worst-case scenarios, protecting balance sheet resilience and covenant compliance precisely when it matters most.

01
Execute 60% hedge at current forward rates
Board to authorise 12-month forward baseload contracts covering 60% of expected generation. Hedge cost sub-CHF 1M/yr; VaR saving exceeds CHF 10M at baseline parameters.
02
Set hard trigger for hedge ratio review
If forward price falls below CHF 40/MWh, convene Treasury and Risk Committee within 5 business days. Do not auto-roll at unfavourable prices.
03
Monitor basis risk monthly
Track actual generation vs hedged volume. If inflow projections fall below 85% of mean, flag to CRO — under-generation creates a short position at the forward price.

Executive recommendation — operational resilience

The simulation reveals that the compound scenario — simultaneous turbine failure and drought year — occurs in approximately 1 in every 8 years at default parameters (P(failure) 58.6% × P(drought) 22% = 12.9% p.a.). This should be treated as a base-case stress scenario, not an extreme outlier. Preventive maintenance delivers the highest VaR reduction per franc spent among the three levers. The TSO standby contract is fastest to implement and most liquidity-efficient. The weather derivative is narrowest in scope but cheapest — a natural add-on once the first two are in place.

01
Approve preventive maintenance CAPEX
Board to authorise CHF 4M/yr programme for turbines exceeding 10 years. Simulation shows VaR saving of CHF 8–15M — a 2–4× return on spend.
02
Negotiate TSO standby contract Q3 2026
Mandate Head of Asset Operations to agree a retainer with the neighbouring TSO by end Q3 2026. CHF 2.5M/yr replaces 35% of lost generation during any outage.
03
Add weather derivative to 2027 hedging
CHF 1.8M premium covers 20% of drought revenue loss — small cost relative to the compound scenario impact and a natural complement to the TSO contract.
04
Elevate compound scenario to Board stress-test
Add simultaneous turbine failure + drought as a named scenario in the annual Board Risk Committee stress-test. Present CVaR — not expected loss — as the basis for capital adequacy decisions.
